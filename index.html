I started writing code that hunted malware. Now I write protocols that let machines earn money.

The gap is smaller than it sounds. Both are about designing systems where correctness isn’t optional — where the rules have to be right because something real depends on them. That problem, in different forms, has driven my work for nearly two decades.

My background is in security. I spent years deep in the internals of malware detection at IKARUS — writing scan engine code, building automated classification algorithms, eventually leading all product development. It was slow, precise, unglamorous work, and it taught me how to think about adversarial systems. By 2015, while still leading development at IKARUS, I was already organizing research funding for a spinoff — bridging applied security research and commercialization two years before Sonar Cyber Intelligence formally launched. When that happened in 2017, I found out what it’s like to simultaneously pitch investors and ship software. That combination — technical depth plus the ability to make a business case — is something I’ve relied on ever since.

In 2018 I joined Riddle&Code as CIO and have been here for eight years now, growing through the roles of Protocol Architect and Senior Architect before becoming CEO. I don’t think of that as a slow climb — I think of it as going genuinely deep on a hard problem. Riddle&Code and the RDDL Foundation are both working on the same question: how do physical machines — sensors, nodes, infrastructure — become first-class, economically active participants in decentralized networks? Right now that question is most alive in energy markets, where the ability to let devices autonomously settle production, consumption, and grid services opens up entirely new market structures. That means getting the cryptography right, getting the token mechanics right, and making sure the whole thing holds together as a business. I find that intersection — where the protocol design and the economic incentives have to be solved together — the most interesting place to work.

If you’re thinking about DePIN, decentralized infrastructure, security architecture, or just want to compare notes on building companies around deep tech — I’m happy to talk.
